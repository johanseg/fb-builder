# Townsquare Interactive -- Creative Copy Framework

**Purpose**: Modular video ad copy system for direct-response campaigns targeting established local service businesses.
**Brand**: Townsquare Interactive -- local marketing and digital presence partner for small businesses.
**Author role**: Senior direct-response creative strategist.
**Delivery format**: Spoken-language video ad scripts. Concise, punchy, native to how local business owners actually talk.

---

## SECTION 1 -- Audience Summary

### Primary ICP

Established local service businesses -- contractors, plumbers, electricians, HVAC techs, roofers, landscapers, painters, remodelers, auto shops, dental offices, law firms, cleaning companies. Owner-operated or small teams. Two or more years in business. $250K+ annual revenue. Two or more employees. Strong reputation built on referrals and word of mouth. Operationally solid but digitally underdeveloped. They do great work -- but their online presence does not reflect that. They are invisible on Google, their website is outdated or nonexistent, and they are losing jobs to competitors who simply show up first in search results.

### Bad-Fit Audience

First-year businesses still figuring out their service offering. Solo operators with no employees and under $250K in revenue. Anyone looking for a silver bullet or expecting instant results from a $200/month spend. Cheapest-option shoppers who treat marketing as a cost center, not an investment. Businesses that need startup launch support -- naming, branding from scratch, first customers. Anyone who needs to be convinced that online visibility matters in the first place.

### Top 8 Pains

1. Losing jobs to competitors who do worse work but show up first on Google.
2. Relying almost entirely on referrals -- which means unpredictable revenue and zero control over lead flow.
3. Getting ghosted by past marketing agencies that took the money and delivered a templated website nobody visits.
4. Having no idea what their Google Business Profile looks like -- or that it even exists.
5. Watching newer, less experienced competitors outrank them in local search because those competitors actually invested in their online presence.
6. Spending money on lead-gen services (Angi, Thumbtack, HomeAdvisor) and competing on price against ten other contractors for the same job.
7. Knowing they need to "do something online" but having zero time, zero expertise, and zero trust in agencies after getting burned.
8. Feeling embarrassed when a customer says "I almost didn't call you -- your website looked sketchy."

### Top 8 Desired Outcomes

1. Showing up first when someone in their area searches for the service they provide.
2. Getting inbound calls and form fills from people who are ready to hire -- not tire-kickers from lead-gen platforms.
3. Having a website that looks as professional as the work they do.
4. Building an online reputation that matches the reputation they already have in person.
5. Owning their lead flow instead of depending on referrals and hoping the phone rings.
6. Having a marketing partner who actually understands local business and does the work for them.
7. Spending marketing dollars on something they can actually measure and see working.
8. Competing with bigger companies without having to become a marketing expert.

### Top 6 Objections

1. "I've been burned by agencies before. They took my money and nothing happened."
2. "I don't have time to deal with marketing stuff. I'm running jobs all day."
3. "We get most of our work from referrals. I'm not sure we need this."
4. "How long before I actually see results?"
5. "I can't afford to spend a lot on marketing right now."
6. "My nephew/friend/employee can build me a website for cheaper."

### Top 5 Strongest Positioning Statements

1. We do not sell marketing promises. We build the infrastructure that makes your business findable, credible, and easy to hire online -- then we manage it so you never have to think about it.
2. You already do the work. We make sure the people searching for that work actually find you -- not the guy down the street who started two years ago but shows up first on Google.
3. We are not a marketing agency that hands you a dashboard and disappears. We are an execution partner. We do the work. Website, SEO, Google Business Profile, content, visibility -- handled.
4. Referrals got you here. But every day, people in your area search Google for exactly what you do, and they hire whoever shows up first. If that is not you, that is revenue walking out the door.
5. This is not about going viral or running flashy ads. This is about making sure that when someone in your zip code types "best plumber near me," your name is the one they see.

## SECTION 2 -- Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1: Visibility Is the Bottleneck

**Statement**: You are not losing jobs because of your work quality. You are losing jobs because people cannot find you.

**Why it matters**: Most established local businesses have never connected the dots between "I'm not getting enough calls" and "I don't show up on Google." They assume the market is slow or competition is tough -- but the real problem is they are invisible in the place where 97% of consumers start their search. This pillar reframes the problem from "marketing doesn't work for me" to "the right people literally cannot find me."

**What not to say**: Do not say "grow your business" or "take your business to the next level." Do not use vague phrases like "increase your online presence." Be specific: you do not show up when someone searches "HVAC repair [city name]." That is the problem. Name it.

---

### Pillar 2: Your Reputation Deserves a Digital Match

**Statement**: You have spent years building a reputation through your work. Your online presence should reflect that -- not undermine it.

**Why it matters**: These business owners take pride in their craft. Their trucks are clean, their work is meticulous, their customers leave happy. But their website looks like it was built in 2012, their Google reviews are sparse, and their online footprint tells a completely different story than the one their customers would tell. This is not about vanity -- it is about credibility. When a potential customer checks you out online before calling, what they find either confirms or kills the referral.

**What not to say**: Do not say "build your brand." These owners do not think in brand terms. They think in reputation terms. Do not imply their business is unprofessional -- acknowledge that their work is excellent and the gap is only digital.

---

### Pillar 3: Done-for-You Execution, Not DIY Tools

**Statement**: You do not need another login. You need someone who does the work.

**Why it matters**: Local business owners have been sold tools, dashboards, DIY website builders, and marketing platforms for years. They do not need more tools. They need execution. They are on job sites, driving trucks, managing crews. The last thing they want is homework from a marketing company. This pillar positions Townsquare as the hands that do the work, not the platform that gives you more to do.

**What not to say**: Do not lead with software features, dashboards, or SaaS capabilities. Do not say "easy-to-use platform." If you mention tools, frame them as things Townsquare manages on their behalf. The owner should feel like they are hiring a crew, not buying a subscription.

---

### Pillar 4: Own Your Leads, Stop Renting Them

**Statement**: Lead-gen platforms rent you access to your own customers. We help you own the pipeline.

**Why it matters**: Many local businesses spend $500-$2,000/month on Angi, Thumbtack, HomeAdvisor -- platforms where they compete against five to ten other contractors for the same lead, often on price alone. When they stop paying, the leads stop. There is no equity. Townsquare builds assets the business owns: a website that ranks, a Google Business Profile that converts, content that compounds. This pillar makes the economic case for infrastructure over rented attention.

**What not to say**: Do not trash lead-gen platforms outright -- many of these owners have gotten real jobs from them. Instead, reframe the economics: "Those platforms work, but you are competing on price with every other contractor in your area. What if people came directly to you instead?"

---

### Pillar 5: Built for Businesses That Already Work

**Statement**: We partner with businesses that are already good at what they do. We make sure the market knows it.

**Why it matters**: This pillar does double duty. First, it flatters the prospect by acknowledging their competence and hard work -- which is genuine, not manipulative. These are skilled tradespeople and business operators. Second, it qualifies out bad fits. If you are a first-year business with no track record, this messaging tells you this is not for you. It signals that Townsquare works with established operators, not startups looking for a Hail Mary.

**What not to say**: Do not say "we help anyone grow." Do not make it sound like Townsquare is for beginners. Avoid startup language like "launch your business" or "get your first customers." The tone is peer-to-peer, not hand-holding.

## SECTION 5 -- Creative Testing Notes

### Pain-Led vs. Aspiration-Led Hook Distribution

| Hook | Type | Emotional Driver |
|------|------|-----------------|
| Hook 1 | PAIN | Invisibility despite tenure and quality |
| Hook 2 | PAIN | Specific lost revenue, named scenario |
| Hook 3 | PAIN | Social proof in reverse -- "almost didn't call" |
| Hook 4 | ASPIRATION | Dominance in local search |
| Hook 5 | PAIN | Competitor comparison, unfairness |
| Hook 6 | ASPIRATION | Pre-sold inbound calls |
| Hook 7 | PAIN | Lead-gen platform economics, price competition |
| Hook 8 | ASPIRATION | Peer comparison, achievable outcome |
| Hook 9 | PAIN | Referral limitation, uncaptured demand |
| Hook 10 | PAIN | Website as liability, not asset |

**Ratio**: 7 pain-led, 3 aspiration-led. Pain hooks will typically outperform on cold traffic. Aspiration hooks work better on warm/retargeting audiences who already understand the problem.

### Platform-Specific Angle Fit

**Facebook**: Best for longer-form pain hooks and economic arguments. The feed allows for more considered engagement. Hooks 2, 5, 7, 9, and 10 test well here because Facebook users scroll slowly and respond to specific, relatable scenarios. Use Core sections that build a full argument (Core 6, Core 7, Core 8).

**YouTube**: Best for credibility and mechanism hooks. Pre-roll requires a strong interrupt in the first five seconds. Hooks 2, 3, and 6 work well because they create immediate tension or curiosity. YouTube allows longer Core sections -- use Core 3, Core 7, and Core 9 where the full story matters. Thirty-second to ninety-second formats perform best.

**TikTok**: Best for punchy, direct-to-camera, fast-paced delivery. Hooks 1, 4, 7, and 8 work because they hit fast and feel like real talk, not polished ads. Keep total script under forty-five seconds. Core sections need to be trimmed to the three strongest lines. CTA should be immediate and direct. Conversational, slightly confrontational tone wins here.

### Best Combinations for Contractors / Home Services

These pairings are specifically calibrated for contractors, plumbers, electricians, HVAC, roofers, and remodelers:

1. **Hook 2 + Bridge 2 + Core 1 + CTA 2** -- The lost job angle. Extremely specific. Names a dollar amount, a distance, a service. This combination performs well because contractors can immediately picture the exact scenario.

2. **Hook 5 + Bridge 1 + Core 5 + CTA 4** -- The competitor angle. Contractors know exactly who their local competitors are. This hits the "that guy gets more work than me and I know I am better" nerve.

3. **Hook 7 + Bridge 6 + Core 6 + CTA 6** -- The Angi/lead-gen escape angle. Every contractor who has used Angi or HomeAdvisor has felt the frustration of competing on price for shared leads. This combination validates that frustration and offers an alternative.

4. **Hook 9 + Bridge 4 + Core 8 + CTA 1** -- The referral ceiling. This works for the more established contractor who has always relied on word of mouth and feels "marketing is not for me." It reframes marketing as capturing demand that already exists.

### Lines That Qualify Out Poor-Fit Prospects

The following lines are designed to subtly filter out businesses that are not a good fit without explicitly rejecting them:

- "We partner with businesses that are already good at what they do." (Core 10) -- filters out pre-revenue and struggling businesses.
- "If you are looking for a miracle, we are not the right fit." (Core 10) -- filters out desperate, unrealistic-expectation prospects.
- "Two or more years in business" -- when used in targeting copy or landing pages, sets a clear floor.
- "We are not in the business of saving struggling companies." (Core 10) -- direct and clear without being condescending.
- "Built for businesses that already work." (Pillar 5 / Core 10) -- repeated framing that positions Townsquare as a growth partner, not a rescue service.

Use Ad 12 (TikTok qualifier ad) and the Core 10 section specifically in retargeting sequences to reduce unqualified booked calls.

### Recommended A/B Test Pairings

**Test 1 -- Hook format**: Hook 2 (specific lost-job scenario) vs. Hook 5 (competitor comparison). Both are pain-led but use different mechanisms. Hook 2 uses a concrete dollar figure. Hook 5 uses ego and fairness. Test on Facebook with the same Bridge, Core, and CTA.

**Test 2 -- Pain vs. aspiration**: Hook 7 (Angi pain) vs. Hook 8 (15-20 calls aspiration). Same target audience, same Bridge 6, same Core 6. This isolates whether the pain or the aspiration framing drives more clicks and booked calls.

**Test 3 -- CTA friction level**: CTA 1 (free visibility audit, soft) vs. CTA 2 (book a call, hard). Run these as the only variable on your best-performing ad. Soft CTAs drive more leads but lower intent. Hard CTAs drive fewer leads but higher close rates. Optimize based on cost-per-qualified-appointment, not cost-per-click.

**Test 4 -- Core mechanism**: Core 1 (local SEO explanation) vs. Core 6 (owned vs. rented lead economics). Both are strong. Core 1 is educational. Core 6 is financial. Test which resonates more with the contractor audience.

**Test 5 -- Platform-native delivery**: Same script (Ad 9) delivered on TikTok in three formats: (a) founder talking to camera, (b) screen-recording of Google search results with voiceover, (c) before/after Google ranking screenshots with text overlay. Same copy, different creative execution. This tests whether the message or the medium drives performance on short-form video.
